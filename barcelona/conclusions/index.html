Conclusions

The integration of multi-year datasets from the Parc Vall Hebron station allows us to draw several critical conclusions about the air we breathe in Barcelona. By transforming complex numerical records into visual patterns, we can clearly see the "pulse" of the city’s pollution.

1ST GRAPHIC The Time Variation analysis reveals a unmistakable link between urban mobility and air quality. The nitrogen oxides (NO,NO2) show sharp peaks during the early morning (8:00 AM) and late afternoon (8:00 PM). This double peak pattern perfectly aligns with the city's rush hours, proving that vehicle emissions are the primary driver of pollution in the area.

Our data shows that air quality is not static. We observe significantly lower levels of pollution during weekends (Sunday being the cleanest day) when commercial activity and commuting drop. Seasonally, pollution tends to intensify during the colder months, likely due to stable weather conditions that trap pollutants closer to the ground, compared to the better-ventilated summer months.

2ND GRAPHIC The Trend Level analysis offers a comprehensive historical view of $NO_2$ fluctuations. By correlating the time of day with seasonal changes, we can identify specific 'pollution hotspots' with high precision. Although certain periods show signs of improvement, the recurring 'red zones' during peak commuting hours across multiple years suggest that high nitrogen dioxide levels remain a structural issue in high-density urban areas like Parc Vall Hebron.

3RD GRAPHIC The Calendar Plot serves as a vital diagnostic tool for understanding the daily air quality dynamics at Parc Vall Hebron. Unlike broader averages, this visualization allows us to identify specific 'pollution episodes' with high precision.By analyzing the daily color intensities, we can observe that high $NO_2$ levels are not distributed randomly; they often cluster during mid-week periods, reflecting the cumulative impact of industrial and commercial traffic. Furthermore, the presence of isolated 'dark cells' (high pollution days) during weekends or holidays points toward specific meteorological stagnation events where air becomes trapped near the surface. This granular, day-by-day perspective is essential for public health planning, as it highlights that even in years with 'good' averages, citizens are still exposed to dangerous short-term spikes in air toxicity.
